🕵️ Phishing

Phishing is a type of Social EngineeringPsychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. where an attacker sends a fraudulent message designed to trick a person into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or OTPs.

Common Tactics

  • Spoofed sender domains — e.g. support@amaz0n.in instead of amazon.in.
  • Urgency & fear — "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours. Act now."
  • Spear PhishingA targeted phishing attack personalised with the victim's name, role, or organisation to appear more convincing. — Personalised attacks using the victim's name or employer to appear legitimate.
  • Lookalike links — URLs that visually resemble real sites but point to attacker-controlled servers.

How to Stay Safe

  • Hover over links before clicking to preview the real destination URL.
  • Check the sender's raw email headers — the "From" display name can be faked.
  • When in doubt, navigate directly to the official website rather than clicking any link.
  • Enable MFAMulti-Factor Authentication — requires a second proof of identity beyond just a password. so stolen passwords alone cannot grant access.

Real-World Cases

  • Twitter Hack (2020): Attackers used spear phishing calls to trick Twitter employees into handing over admin credentials, compromising accounts of Barack Obama, Elon Musk, and Apple to run a Bitcoin scam.
  • Google & Facebook (2013–2015): A Lithuanian man phished both companies out of $100 million by impersonating a hardware vendor via fake invoices sent over email.

📱 Smishing

Smishing (SMS PhishingPhishing attacks delivered via SMS/text messages instead of email.) is a cyberattack that uses deceptive text messages to trick victims into revealing sensitive information or clicking malicious links. Because SMS bypasses email filters entirely, it reaches victims directly on their most personal device.

Common Tactics

  • Fake delivery alerts — "Your package is held. Verify here: [link]"
  • Bank fraud warnings — "Unusual activity detected. Confirm your details immediately."
  • Prize scams — "You've won! Claim your reward now."
  • Government impersonation — Fake tax refund or fine payment messages with urgent deadlines.

How to Stay Safe

  • Never click links in unsolicited SMS messages — go directly to the official app or website.
  • Verify by calling the organisation directly using an official number, not one provided in the message.
  • Report smishing attempts to your carrier by forwarding the message to 7726 (SPAM).
  • Be especially suspicious of messages creating urgency or threatening consequences.

Real-World Cases

  • FluBot (2021): An Android smishing campaign that spread across Europe by sending fake parcel delivery SMS messages. Clicking the link installed malware that harvested banking credentials and sent itself to the victim's contacts.
  • USPS/FedEx Scam Wave (ongoing): One of the most reported smishing types globally — fake delivery failure messages with links to credential-harvesting sites impersonating major couriers.

📷 Quishing

Quishing (QR Code PhishingPhishing attacks that embed malicious URLs inside QR codes to bypass email/link scanners.) uses malicious QR codes to redirect victims to fake websites designed to steal credentials or install malware.

Why It's Dangerous

  • QR codes are unreadable to the human eye — you can't spot a malicious URL before scanning.
  • Security tools that scan text-based links often cannot inspect QR code content.
  • Attackers place fake QR code stickers over legitimate ones in public spaces (parking meters, restaurant menus, posters).
  • QR codes in emails bypass traditional link-scanning filters used by corporate email gateways.

How to Stay Safe

  • Use a QR scanner that previews the destination URL before opening it.
  • Inspect physical QR codes for stickers placed over the original.
  • Avoid scanning QR codes received in unexpected emails or messages.
  • If a QR code leads to a login page, navigate to the site manually instead.

Real-World Cases

  • Microsoft Credential Theft Campaign (2023): Attackers sent emails containing QR codes to corporate employees. The codes bypassed email security filters and redirected victims to fake Microsoft login pages to harvest credentials.
  • Parking Meter Scams (US, 2022): The FBI issued a warning after fake QR code stickers were placed over legitimate payment QR codes on parking meters across multiple US cities, redirecting payments to attacker-controlled sites.

🦠 Ransomware

Ransomware is a type of MalwareMalicious software designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorised access to a computer system. that encrypts a victim's files and demands payment — usually in CryptocurrencyA digital currency that uses cryptography for security, making transactions difficult to trace (e.g. Bitcoin, Monero). — in exchange for the decryption key.

How an Attack Unfolds

  • Delivery: Arrives via phishing email, malicious attachment, or compromised website.
  • Execution: Malware silently installs and begins encrypting files across the system and network shares.
  • Ransom Demand: A note appears demanding payment within a deadline, threatening permanent data loss.
  • Double Extortion: Modern ransomware groups also exfiltrate data and threaten to publish it publicly if the ransom isn't paid.

Incident Response Steps

  • Isolate — Immediately disconnect the infected machine from the network to stop lateral spread.
  • Eradicate — Use verified, offline security tools to remove the malware. Never download tools from the internet during an active infection.
  • Recover — Restore data from secure, offline backups. Do not pay the ransom — payment does not guarantee recovery and funds further attacks.
  • Report — Notify relevant authorities (e.g. CERT, law enforcement) and affected stakeholders.

How to Stay Safe

  • Maintain regular, Offline BackupsBackups stored on a device not connected to the network, making them immune to ransomware encryption. tested for restoration.
  • Keep all software and operating systems patched and up to date.
  • Disable macros in Office documents received from unknown sources.
  • Use endpoint detection and response (EDR) tools to catch suspicious behaviour early.

Real-World Cases

  • WannaCry (2017): Exploited an unpatched Windows vulnerability (EternalBlue) to infect over 200,000 systems across 150 countries, including the UK's NHS, causing an estimated $4 billion in damages.
  • Colonial Pipeline (2021): A ransomware attack by DarkSide forced the shutdown of the largest fuel pipeline in the US, causing fuel shortages across the East Coast. The company paid $4.4 million in ransom.

🔐 Cryptography

Cryptography is the practice of securing information by transforming it into an unreadable format. It underpins nearly every security control — from password storage to encrypted web traffic. Key concepts include HashingA one-way mathematical function that converts data into a fixed-length string. Cannot be reversed, only compared., EncryptionA two-way process that scrambles data using a key. The data can be unscrambled (decrypted) with the correct key., and EntropyA measure of randomness or unpredictability in a password or key. Higher entropy = harder to guess..

Key Concepts

  • SHA-256A cryptographic hash function producing a fixed 256-bit hash. Used in password storage, digital signatures, and blockchain.: A one-way hash function used to store passwords securely. The same input always produces the same hash, but the hash cannot be reversed.
  • SaltingAdding a unique random value to each password before hashing, preventing rainbow table attacks.: A random value added to a password before hashing, ensuring two identical passwords produce different hashes.
  • bcrypt / Argon2: Modern password hashing algorithms designed to be deliberately slow, making brute-force attacks computationally expensive.
  • TLS/HTTPS: Encrypts data in transit between your browser and a web server, preventing interception.

How to Stay Safe

  • Use passwords with high EntropyEntropy measures unpredictability. A 12-character random password has far higher entropy than a common word. — long, random, and unique per site.
  • Never store or transmit passwords in plaintext — always use a salted hash.
  • Verify HTTPS (padlock icon) before entering credentials on any website.
  • Use a password manager to generate and store high-entropy passwords.

Real-World Cases

  • LinkedIn Breach (2012): 117 million passwords were stored as unsalted SHA-1 hashes. Without salting, identical passwords produced identical hashes, allowing bulk cracking via rainbow tables within days.
  • Adobe Breach (2013): 153 million records leaked with passwords encrypted (not hashed) using 3DES without salting — a fundamental cryptographic error that made mass decryption trivial.

🎭 Social Engineering

Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information. Unlike technical attacks, it exploits human trust, fear, and authority rather than software vulnerabilities. Attackers often use PretextingCreating a fabricated scenario (the pretext) to trick a victim into providing information or taking an action. to establish credibility before making their request.

Common Tactics

  • Pretexting — Impersonating IT support, a bank, or a colleague to create a believable reason for the request.
  • BaitingLeaving infected USB drives or offering fake downloads to lure victims into executing malware. — Leaving infected USB drives in car parks or offering fake software downloads.
  • Quid Pro QuoOffering a service (e.g. free IT help) in exchange for credentials or access. — Offering a service (e.g. free tech support) in exchange for login credentials.
  • Authority & Urgency — Claiming to be a senior executive or regulator and demanding immediate action to bypass rational thinking.
  • TailgatingPhysically following an authorised person through a secure door without using credentials. — Physically following an authorised person into a restricted area.

How to Stay Safe

  • IT support will never ask for your password — over any channel.
  • Always verify identity through a secondary, known channel before acting on any request.
  • Treat urgency as a red flag — attackers manufacture time pressure to prevent you from thinking critically.
  • Follow a clear process for verifying financial or access requests, regardless of who is asking.

Real-World Cases

  • Uber Breach (2022): An attacker posed as IT support and bombarded an employee with MFA push notifications until they approved one out of fatigue, then used social engineering to gain access to internal admin tools.
  • MGM Resorts (2023): Attackers called the IT help desk, impersonated an employee using information from LinkedIn, and convinced staff to reset MFA — leading to a breach estimated at $100 million in damages.

🔐 Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) requires a user to provide two or more independent proofs of identity before gaining access. Even if an attacker steals your password, they cannot log in without the second factor. The three factor categories are: Something You KnowA password, PIN, or security question answer., Something You HaveA physical device such as a phone (for OTP apps) or a hardware security key., and Something You AreA biometric identifier such as a fingerprint, face scan, or voice recognition..

MFA Methods (Weakest to Strongest)

  • SMS OTP — A one-time code sent via text. Convenient but vulnerable to SIM SwappingAn attack where a criminal convinces a mobile carrier to transfer your phone number to their SIM card, intercepting your SMS codes..
  • Authenticator App (TOTP) — Time-based codes generated by an app (e.g. Google Authenticator). Stronger than SMS as codes never leave your device.
  • Push Notification — A prompt sent to your phone to approve or deny. Vulnerable to MFA FatigueAn attack where the attacker sends repeated MFA push requests hoping the victim approves one out of frustration or confusion. attacks.
  • Hardware Security Key (FIDO2/Passkey) — A physical USB or NFC key. Phishing-resistant and the strongest form of MFA available.

How to Stay Safe

  • Enable MFA on every account that supports it — especially email, banking, and work accounts.
  • Prefer an authenticator app or hardware key over SMS where possible.
  • Never approve an MFA push notification you did not initiate — deny and report it immediately.
  • If you receive repeated unexpected MFA prompts, treat it as an active attack in progress.

Real-World Cases

  • Uber (2022): An attacker used MFA fatigue — sending repeated push notifications until an employee approved one — to bypass MFA entirely without ever stealing the OTP code.
  • Twilio / Okta (2022): SMS-based MFA codes were intercepted after attackers SIM-swapped employees' phone numbers, demonstrating why SMS OTP is the weakest MFA method.
